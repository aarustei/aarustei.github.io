Autonomous Robotic Grasping for On-orbit Satellite Repair

Path planning and controller design in MATLAB of a robotic arm that grasps spinning satellites in orbit

Introduction

Humanity’s need for radio communication, weather monitoring, and navigation makes near-Earth outer space an important natural resource exploited by satellites [1, 2]. Rapid growth in satellite deployments increases demand for on-orbit servicing (repair, refueling, maintenance, and debris mitigation) [3, 4]. Human servicing missions are costly and risky, but robotic systems provide safer and more scalable alternatives. Autonomous grasping of non-cooperative, possibly rotating targets remains a key technical challenge in space robotics.

Project Goal

  • Implement a framework for a robotic system capable of:
    • Planning interception trajectories to a moving and spinning target,
    • Tracking planned motion using a feedback controller,
    • Grasping the target and evaluating grasp quality based on quantitative metrics.

High-level Approach

The following ideas were combined:

  • Simulation environment: MATLAB/Simulink and Simscape Multibody.
  • Manipulator: Kinova Jaco® 7-DOF robotic arm with a three-finger gripper and spherical wrist from Matlab’s Robotics System Toolbox.
  • Motion planning: Joint-space RRT with B-spline interpolation for smooth trajectories.
  • Controller: Computed-torque control for joint tracking.
  • Grasp evaluation: Minimum singular value of the grasp matrix \(G\) [5,6].
Methodology details

Controller Design

We implemented a Computed Torque Controller which is based on the feedback linearization idea for nonlinear systems [7]. The input-output linearization makes the control signal cancel the nonlinear terms in the equations of motion to create a linear error model. Consider a robot described by \begin{equation} M(q)\ddot{q} + C(q,\dot{q})\dot{q} + G(q) = u \,, \end{equation} where \(q\) are the generalized coordinates, \(M(q)\) is the mass matrix, \(C(q,\dot{q})\) is the Coriolis matrix, \(G(q)\) is the gravity torque (in our case zero for a robot in space). If the inputs (commanded joint torques) are defined by \begin{equation} u = M(q)(\ddot{q}_d - K_d \dot{q}_e - K_p q_e) + C(q,\dot{q})\dot{q} + G(q) \,, \end{equation} where the error is \(q_e = q - q_d\) with \(q_d\) being the desired positions to be tracked, then the error dynamics become \begin{equation} \ddot{q}_e + K_d \dot{q}_e + K_p q_e = 0 \,. \end{equation} By choosing the gains \(K_d\) and \(K_e\) positive, the error dynamics become stable which means the joint angles \(q\) and its derivatives will follow the desired values \(q_d\). Path Planning

The desired trajectory to be tracked is computed by the path planner block (see Figure 16), which acts as a higher-level control entity. To generate a reference trajectory, we employed the Rapidly-exploring Random Tree (RRT) algorithm [8], which samples random states in the joint space to find a feasible path connecting the start and goal poses while avoiding collisions. It must be emphasized that this is a simplified problem that only seeks a feasible series of joint positions \(q\) that connects the initial and target configurations of the robot, disregarding the time or joint velocities. This geometric problem only requires solving the kinematics repeatedly and checking for collisions. Dexterous Space Study

To determine when and where the robotic arm should attempt a grasp, one can take into account the dexterous workspace of the manipulator. Defined as the space in which the manipulator can reach in any position, the dexterous workspace can be calculated by repeatedly solving the inverse kinematics problem on sampled points in the workspace. For each position, a number of 26 different orientations are attempted, taking into account the rotational symmetries of the robot. In Figure 18, a contour plot of the dexterity is shown, measured by how many orientations could be reached at every point. Based on this metric, the preferable region for target capture is around (0.4 m, 0.25 m) from the base in the vertical plane.

Results

In the closed-loop simulation, the target object was initialized with a velocity such that it reached the robot’s workspace after 5 seconds. The robot successfully tracked, intercepted, and grasped the object.

Conclusion

We successfully designed a framework for a robotic system capable of tracking and grasping a potentially spinning client in a space environment using an RRT path planner and a computed torque controller. The model incorporates the dynamics of the entire robotic arm. The arm is capable of grasping small, free-floating objects. The grasp stability was analyzed based on the minimum singular value of the grasp matrix.

A current limitation is that the robot does not automatically reconfigure its grasp if the initial attempt is unstable. More planning logic can be added to address this, as well as more grasp metrics. The equations of motion can also be expanded to include relative dynamics or non-inertial terms.

This project was part of the Robotics and Control course at WSU and was completed in a team of two. I was responsible for the simulations and controller design shown above.
