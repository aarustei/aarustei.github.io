Inverted Pendulum on a Cart

December 8, 2021 • 5 min read •
Tags:
  • MATLAB
  • Control Theory
  • Linear Systems

Design of a feedback loop for the stabilization of an inverted pendulum on a cart

Project Goal

The objective of this project is to design a state feedback controller of the form \( u(t) = -K x(t) + f r(t) \) in order to balance an inverted pendulum while tracking the reference cart position within the given constraints. A schematic of the system is shown in Figure 1.

Model and Assumptions

The linearized dynamics of the inverted pendulum take the form of a linear time-invariant system \begin{equation} \label{eq:linsyst} \begin{aligned} \dot{x}(t) &= A x(t) + B u(t) \\ y(t) &= C x(t) + D u(t) \end{aligned} \end{equation}

where the state variables \( x(t) = [x_c(t)\; \alpha(t)\; \dot{x}_c(t)\; \dot{\alpha}(t) ]\) are comprised by the cart’s position, angle, horizontal velocity, and angular rate, respectively. The input \( u(t) \) is the motor voltage of the cart, our control variable. The system matrices are given. Only the position and angle are measured. However, all states are assumed to be available for the feedback law.

Controller Design

The matrices \(K\) and \( f \) are calculated using the pole placement method. That is, a desired characteristic equation for the closed-loop system has to be specified. The system is a 4th order one, so the characteristic polynomial will have 4 roots. However, the usual formulas for transient characteristics (settling time, rise time, etc.) are only valid for second-order systems. In this case, if the 4th order system has a pair of dominant poles and two poles far in the left side of the complex plane, then the system will behave approximately as a second-order system. This is known as the dominant pole approximation.

We have chosen the desired percentage overshoot to be \(PO_d = 1\%\) and the desired settling time \( {T_s}_d = 2.4 s\). From these two values, the damping ratio and natural frequency can be calculated as follows: \begin{align} \zeta &= \frac{-\ln \frac{P O_d}{100}}{\sqrt{\pi^2+\left(\ln \frac{P O_d}{100}\right)^2}}=\frac{-\ln 0.01}{\sqrt{\pi^2+(\ln 0.01)^2}}=0.8261 \\ \omega_n &= \frac{-\ln \left(0.02 \sqrt{1-\zeta^2}\right)}{\zeta T_{s_d}} =\frac{-\ln \left(0.02 \sqrt{1-0.82^2}\right)}{0.82 \times 2.4}=2.2624 \\ \sigma_d &= \omega_n \zeta=1.8690 \\ \omega_d &= \omega_n \sqrt{1-\zeta^2}=1.2750 \end{align}

Then, the characteristic equation can be built. If we choose the other two poles to be located at 6 and 8 times the real part of the dominant poles, then the desired characteristic equation becomes \begin{equation} \Delta_d(s)=\left(s+\sigma_d-j \omega_d\right)\left(s+\sigma_d+j \omega_d\right)\left(s+6 \sigma_d\right)\left(s+8 \sigma_d\right) ,. \end{equation}

In Matlab one can use the command K = place(A,B,[desired_poles]) to calculate the matrix \(K\) such that the closed-loop poles of \(A_{cl}=A-BK\) are in the desired location. This yields the following values for \(K\) and \(f\): \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} & K=\left[\begin{array}{llll} -16.67 & 64.57 & -21.80 & 12.11 \end{array}\right] \\ & f=-\left(C_1 A_{c l}^{-1} B\right)^{-1}=-16.67 \end{aligned} \end{equation} Note that the gain \(f\) is a scalar that multiplies a single reference input \(r(t)\). Therefore \(C_1\) is the first row of matrix \(C\) which corresponds to the desired output to be tracked (in this case the cart position, \(x_c\), which is the first state variable). A block diagram of the closed-loop system is shown in Figure 2.

Experimental Results

The experimental setup (as shown in Figure 6 or setup manual [2]) was able to stabilize the pendulum with the provided control law (Figure 7). However, the cart position presented large overshoots and steady-state errors. Those come from the unmodeled dynamics like cable friction, electric motor dynamics, system nonlinearities, wind gust disturbances (from the experimenters moving in the room), and so on. An integral term might be needed in the control law to drive the steady-state errors to zero.

It can be noticed that even though the cart position has large overshoots, the pendulum angle is kept within small bounds.

Conclusion

A full state feedback law was successfully designed and tested to balance an inverted pendulum on a cart while tracking a certain cart position. A simple linearized model was sufficient for the design purpose. The experimental data shows that the pendulum angle has small deviations from the equilibrium position, but the cart has large overshoots compared to the reference signal. These come from the unmodeled dynamics and other disturbances.

This project was part of the Linear Systems course at WSU and was completed in a team of three. I was responsible for the simulations and controller design shown above. The project also included observability and controllability tests, as well as an observer design, not detailed here.
