About Me

During middle school and high school, I used to participate in various mathematics and physics competitions organized at the local level (school- or city-wise). I also used to attend some extracurricular lessons in physics held rotatively by teachers from different schools in my hometown. Whenever a contest’s profile was more creative, I scored well and received small awards and even qualified for the contest’s national phase. These activities instilled competitiveness in me from a young age. In college I have continued participating in the student contests for strength of materials, mechanics, and mathematics with national-level results. It turned out that all these years were excellent training for what was about to be the biggest challenge of my professional life: completing a PhD.

As a PhD student in aerospace engineering at Wichita State University, I became interested in astrodynamics, low-thrust trajectory optimization, and optimal control theory. However, as the complexity of the problems increased, teamwork became essential to tackle the technical challenges. Fostering collaborations with researchers at Kansas State University or with other professors in our department, not only with my advisor, I was able to quickly learn and bring contributions to the field. From experience, my remark is that competition is valuable for sharpening one’s skills and determination, but ultimately it is collaboration that enables sustained progress, deeper understanding, and impactful scientific contributions. This observation invites reflection on the current academic system increasingly shaped by competition for funding and recognition.
