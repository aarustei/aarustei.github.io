Symbolic Regression for a Non-elementary Integral

Closed-form approximations can be discovered for integrals without solutions in terms of elementary functions through symbolic regression

What is symbolic regression?

Consider the task of predicting the temperature of a cup of tea over time based on a few measurements. Estimating the relationship between a dependent variable and an independent variable is called regression analysis. These types of problems are very common in engineering, statistics, economics, etc. The simplest regression is a linear regression, which answers the following question: what line passes the closest to some given data points? Note how the question already made an assumption, namely that the fitted model is linear, that is, a line. But what if the model structure is unknown and we are free to choose any curve for fitting? How do we choose one? The control theory-related field that studies how to build models from measured inputs and outputs of a system is called system identification. However, if we purposefully seek models with closed-form, simple, mathematical expressions, then we are in the realm of symbolic regression, a topic more closely related to computer science.

The Fresnel integral

Usually appearing in physics, the integral of sine of x squared is called a Fresnel Integral and may have slightly different definitions. Since there is no closed-form solution in terms of elementary functions (such as polynomials, exponentials, logarithms), we will consider the task of approximating \begin{equation} S(x) := \int_0^x \sin(t^2) dt \,. \end{equation}

A couple of very clever approximations have been devised by (Prince, 2022), of which the formula \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} A_1(x) &:= a \cdot \tanh (x)+\frac{1}{2 x} \cdot\left(e^{-x^2}-\cos \left(x^2\right)\right) \\ &\approx \int \sin \left(x^2\right) d x \end{aligned} \end{equation} is particularly intriguing due to its low error and simplicity.

The root mean square error of \(A_1(x)\) for an inteval of interest \( -10<x<10 \) is \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} RMSE_1 &= \sqrt{\frac{1}{20}\int_{-10}^{10}\left(S(x) - A_1(x)\right)^2 dx} \\ &= 0.6083 \,. \end{aligned} \end{equation}

Can we come up with a better formula?

It ought to be emphasized the challenge of searching the discrete space of possible combinations of elementary functions, as well as the continuous space of any constants appearing in a mathematical expression. There is an active area of research proposing all sorts of algorithms (including machine learning) that exploit properties in equations such as symmetries, consistency for units of measurement, and sometimes the ability to generate large amounts of accurate data.

A common approach is to formalize the construction of a mathematical formula through a binary tree (Figure 2) so that it is easier to manipulate. The idea is that the tree of operations can be made to evolve through various genetic algorithms or other heuristics such that the expression evaluates as close as possible to a given dataset.

To demonstrate the tehnique we shall employ a very well implemented open-source software called HeuristicLab, specialized in evolutionary algorithms/genetic programmming.

Result

After a retuning of parameters, we found a new approximation \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} A_2(x) &:= \left(\tanh(c_1 x^3) + \frac{\tanh(c_2 x) \cos(x^2) \tanh(c_3 x^3)}{c_4 x} \right) c_5 \\ &\approx \int \sin \left(x^2\right) d x \end{aligned} \end{equation} where the constants have the values \begin{equation} \begin{bmatrix} c_1\\ c_2\\ c_3\\ c_4\\ c_5 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}0.7120\\ 1.1521\\ -0.3298\\ 1.2428\\ 0.6277\end{bmatrix} \,. \end{equation} How good is it? Well, the RMSE becomes \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} RMSE_2 &= \sqrt{\frac{1}{20}\int_{-10}^{10}\left(S(x) - A_2(x)\right)^2 dx} \\ &= 0.0054 \,, \end{aligned} \end{equation} which is two orders of magnitude smaller than the previous one. Final remarks

A new approximation for the Fresnel integral has been found with a lower root mean square error and with a small expression length and depth. Genetic programming was the indispensable tool for searching the model space. The world of science really needs transparent, human-readable, interpretable mathematical models!
