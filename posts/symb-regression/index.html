Closed-form approximations can be discovered for integrals without solutions in terms of elementary functions through symbolic regression

What is symbolic regression?

Consider the task of predicting the temperature of a cup of tea over time based on a few measurements. Estimating the relationship between a dependent variable and an independent variable is called regression analysis. These types of problems are very common in engineering, statistics, economics, etc. The simplest regression is a linear regression, which answers the following question: what line passes the closest to the given data points? Note how the question already made an assumption, namely that the fitted model is linear, that is, a line. But what if the model structure is unknown and we are free to choose any curve for fitting? How do we choose one? The control theory-related field that studies how to build models from measured inputs and outputs of a system is called system identification. However, if we purposefully seek models with closed-form, simple, mathematical expressions, then we are in the realm of symbolic regression, a topic more closely related to computer science.

The Fresnel Integral

Usually appearing in physics, the integral of sine of x squared is called a Fresnel Integral and may have slightly different definitions. Since there is no closed-form solution in terms of elementary functions (such as polynomials, exponentials, logarithms), we will consider the task to approximate \begin{equation} S(u) := \int_0^u \sin(x^2) dx \,. \end{equation}

A couple of very clever approximations have been devised by (Prince, 2022), of which the formula \begin{equation} a \cdot \tanh (x)+\frac{1}{2 x} \cdot\left(e^{-x^2}-\cos \left(x^2\right)\right) \approx \int \sin \left(x^2\right) d x \end{equation} is particularly intriguing due to its low error and simplicity.
